CASI x Gray Swan Jailbreaking Hackathon

Sunday, November 23, 2025 | 12-7PM EST

About Gray Swan

Gray Swan is an established AI safety and security company, founded by CMU professors and PhD student Andy Zou. Gray Swan hosts arenas: competitions to see who can achieve the most successful attacks across different environments in a set period of time. Right now, Gray Swan is hosting an (1) Indirect Prompt Injection arena and (2) Machine-in-the-Middle arena.

What's jailbreaking?

To jailbreak a model is to elicit from it behavior that is otherwise restricted/unintended by its servicer's guardrails. In an indirect prompt injection scenario, this could look like inserting malicious content (e.g. instructions to edit a user's files) into a webpage. Now, when a model using web-search scrapes the page for context at inference-time, it might behave adversarially toward the user that gave it a simple, benign prompt. Your task in the Indirect Prompt Injection arena is to sneakily elicit these malicious behaviors via prompts.

The Machine-in-the-Middle arena is not a jailbreaking arena. It is a more traditional CTF/cybersecurity competition in which you will use AI to assist you to break into computer systems. Gray Swan is interested in measuring capability uplift: how AI changes offensive security operations in practice.

So what's the event?

Next Sunday, 11/23, 12-7PM EST, CASI is hosting a hackathon for these two arenas. This is dedicated time for you or your team to attack! Gray Swan is offering $140,000 in prizes across the two arenas; in addition, CASI is providing a guaranteed pool of $3,000 to be awarded at the end of the day based on total successful attacks. In addition, Gray Swan offers red-team contracting opportunities and fast-tracked interviews for top arena performers.

Featured Talk

We will be hosting a talk from Eliot Jones, Head of Offensive Security at Gray Swan.

Measuring and Mitigating Cybersecurity Risks of State-of-the-Art AI Agents

Measuring the long-term cybersecurity risks posed by AI agents is an incredibly difficult problem. On one hand, lackluster performance by frontier models on the industry standard cybersecurity evaluations would suggest that offensive security capabilities aren't strong enough to be dangerous. On the other hand, there is a massive gap between evaluating the performance of language models versus the performance of state-of-the-art AI agents. Understanding whether we're measuring meaningful risk indicators or merely correlated proxies has significant implications for how we prepare for and respond to potential threats.

In this talk, we will examine how to measure and respond to long-term cybersecurity risks from AI agents. First, we will explore and discuss what threat models are convincing here, and what threat models are actually being measured. Second, we will compare current evaluation methods against our expectations for what a "good evaluation" should be. Lastly, we will discuss how to mitigate these risks in practice, examining proposed safeguards and the tradeoffs they introduce.

Gray Swan's global leaderboard is designed for individual competitors. Allowing multi-person teams to use personal accounts would create an unfair advantage.

Therefore:

  • Team accounts are event-only and not eligible for Gray Swan global prizes.
  • Individual accounts can compete normally.

Since the event uses two different leaderboards (Indirect Prompt Injection and Machine-in-the-Middle), we compute each team's overall rank by converting ranks on each leaderboard into a normalized score and then averaging those scores. This is equivalent to using Borda count, a voting method that satisfies neutrality, anonymity, monotonicity, and consistency.

1. Normalize ranks within each leaderboard

Suppose a leaderboard has n teams. Let ri be the rank of team i on that leaderboard (with ri = 1 being best and ri = n being worst).

We convert ranks to a normalized score:

ŝi = 1 − (ri − 1) / (n − 1)

This maps the top-performing team to ŝi = 1, the lowest-performing team to ŝi = 0, and all other teams linearly in between. Each leaderboard is normalized independently.

2. Average the normalized scores across leaderboards

If there are two leaderboards, A and B, then each team's final combined score is:

FinalScore(i) = (ŝiA + ŝiB) / 2

A team's overall ranking is determined by sorting teams in descending order of FinalScore(i).

Notes:

  • Teams may perform differently across arenas due to difficulty, familiarity, or topic differences.
  • Both leaderboards contribute equally to the final ranking.
  • If a team only participates in one leaderboard, its score on the other leaderboard will be treated as the lowest (i.e., normalized score = 0).
